# Parkinson's Research

Daily research for families navigating Parkinson's.

## Clinical Trials

### PD GENEration Study Enrolls Across the U.S.
The Parkinson's Foundation's flagship genetic study, PD GENEration, continues to recruit participants nationwide. The study offers free genetic testing and counseling to people with Parkinson's, with the goal of identifying genetic markers that may guide future treatment choices. Participation helps move the field toward personalized medicine while giving participants actionable information about their own diagnosis. Enrollment is open at parkinson.org/join-study.
*From: Parkinson's Foundation* (https://www.parkinson.org/advancing-research/join-study)

### Ambroxol Trial Tests Brain-Clearing Drug in Early PD
The GREAT trial (GRoningen Early-PD Ambroxol Treatment) is recruiting people with early-stage Parkinson's who carry a GBA gene mutation — a common genetic risk factor. Ambroxol, a cough medicine repurposed as a "molecular chaperone," boosts an enzyme called GCase that helps clear cellular garbage, including the toxic alpha-synuclein proteins that build up in Parkinson's brains. If it works, this approach could help not just mutation carriers but potentially everyone with PD. The trial (NCT05830396) runs for 12 months with motor and non-motor symptom assessments.
*From: APDP* (https://www.apdaparkinson.org/article/new-pd-treatments-clinical-trial-pipeline/)

### Gene Therapy AAV2-GDNF Advances to Phase II
A new Phase II trial (NCT06285643) is evaluating AAV2-GDNF, an investigational gene therapy that delivers a growth factor gene directly into the putamen region of the brain. The goal is to stimulate long-term production of GDNF, a protein that supports and protects the dopamine neurons lost in Parkinson's. This approach could provide lasting neuroprotection without daily medications. Phase I data confirmed the surgical delivery is safe.
*From: ClinicalTrials.gov / APDP* (https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT06285643)

## Breakthrough Treatments

### Prasinezumab Moves to Phase III — The Biggest Alpha-Synuclein News Yet
Roche's prasinezumab, an antibody designed to bind and neutralize toxic alpha-synuclein proteins, is advancing to Phase III clinical trials. The decision follows 2.5-year data showing that people on stable levodopa who received prasinezumab declined about 40% more slowly than those on placebo — essentially gaining roughly four months of delayed progression. In the levodopa subgroup, the benefit was statistically significant, and brain imaging showed preserved neuromelanin (a marker of dopamine neuron health). If Phase III succeeds, prasinezumab could become one of the first disease-modifying drugs approved for Parkinson's.
*From: The Michael J. Fox Foundation* (https://www.michaeljfox.org/news/drug-potential-slow-or-stop-parkinsons-moves-next-phase-clinical-testing)

### Tavapadon Completes Phase III, Seeks FDA Approval
Tavapadon, an oral once-daily dopamine agonist, has completed three Phase III trials showing it is safe and effective both as a standalone treatment for newly diagnosed Parkinson's and as an add-on therapy for those with motor fluctuations. In the add-on study, participants gained roughly one extra hour of good "on" time per day without increased dyskinesias. The manufacturer has stated plans to submit the drug to the FDA for regulatory approval.
*From: The Michael J. Fox Foundation* (https://www.michaeljfox.org/news/sleep-diet-and-new-treatments-featured-annual-neurology-conference)

### Cell Therapy Bemdaneprocel Skips Phase II, Heads to Phase III
Bemdaneprocel, an experimental cell therapy that replaces lost dopamine-producing neurons, showed 24-month Phase I data with no safety concerns and positive trends in motor outcomes. The FDA granted permission to skip Phase II and proceed directly to Phase III — a significant vote of confidence. This therapy is surgically implanted into the brain and aims to do what transplants have long promised: genuinely replace the cells Parkinson's destroys.
*From: The Michael J. Fox Foundation* (https://www.michaeljfox.org/news/sleep-diet-and-new-treatments-featured-annual-neurology-conference)

## Lifestyle Interventions

### Treating Sleep Apnea May Slow Parkinson's Progression
A landmark 20-year study of millions of veterans found that people with obstructive sleep apnea who started CPAP treatment within two years of diagnosis had significantly lower rates of Parkinson's five years later compared to those who delayed treatment. The finding was presented at the 2025 American Academy of Neurology conference and adds sleep apnea to the short list of modifiable risk factors in Parkinson's. Researchers encourage proactive screening and treatment of sleep apnea in people with or at risk for PD.
*From: The Michael J. Fox Foundation* (https://www.michaeljfox.org/news/sleep-diet-and-new-treatments-featured-annual-neurology-conference)

### Mediterranean and MIND Diets Backed by Growing Evidence
At the same neurology conference, movement disorder specialists highlighted research supporting the Mediterranean and MIND diets for managing Parkinson's symptoms. Both diets are rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and healthy fats. For people with motor fluctuations, a practical tip from experts: shift most protein to dinner and limit it at breakfast and lunch, which can improve how well levodopa is absorbed during the day. Staying hydrated and eating high-fiber foods also helps manage constipation, a common non-motor symptom.
*From: The Michael J. Fox Foundation* (https://www.michaeljfox.org/news/sleep-diet-and-new-treatments-featured-annual-neurology-conference)

## Emerging Research

### Active Immunotherapy ACI-7104 Shows Early Promise
AC Immune's experimental vaccine ACI-7104.056 became the second alpha-synuclein-targeting immunotherapy to show signals of slowing Parkinson's progression, following prasinezumab. Interim Phase II data from the VacSYn trial showed stabilization of key disease-related biomarkers in people with early Parkinson's. This is notable because directly targeting alpha-synuclein — the misfolded protein that forms Lewy bodies — has been a major research focus for decades, with many previous disappointments.
*From: AlzForum* (https://www.alzforum.org/news/conference-coverage/does-synuclein-immunotherapy-slow-parkinsons-after-all)

### AI-Designed Parkinson's Drug ISM8969 Eyes Clinical Trials
Insilico Medicine is developing ISM8969, an oral therapy designed using artificial intelligence to block harmful brain inflammation linked to Parkinson's progression. In preclinical mouse studies, the drug restored motor skills. The company planned to file for FDA approval to begin human clinical trials by the end of 2025. If it enters trials, it would be among the first AI-designed drugs to enter clinical testing for Parkinson's.
*From: Parkinson Association* (https://parkinsonassociation.org/whats-new-in-parkinsons-breakthroughs-studies-and-support/)

### Brain Inflammation Drugs Attract Major Investment
Five different drugs targeting the NLRP3 inflammasome — a key driver of chronic brain inflammation in Parkinson's — are now in various stages of clinical testing. These include Inzomelid, NT-0796 (which completed a Phase 1b/2a showing dose-dependent reductions in inflammatory markers), VTX3232, VENT-02, and dapansutrile. Unlike symptomatic drugs, these aim to slow or stop disease progression by calming the neuroinflammation that accelerates neuron loss.
*From: APDP* (https://www.apdaparkinson.org/article/new-pd-treatments-clinical-trial-pipeline/)
